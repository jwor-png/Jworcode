# Voice — How John Writes and Sounds

## Observed Patterns (from briefs and instructions)
- Direct. Gets to the point without preamble.
- Thinks in structures — lists, numbered sections, named outputs.
- Commands read like instructions, not requests: "Start with what you know. Mark anything you're guessing. Ask me the gaps."
- Short sentences. No padding.
- Mixes lowercase informality with formal structure — comfortable toggling between the two.
- Thinks fast, often dictates — phonetic/spoken patterns appear in writing.
- Frames ideas as positions to be acted on, not options to be considered.

## Tone
- Warm but professional
- Peer-to-peer — not sales language, not corporate language
- Precision over volume
- Confident and decisive
- Genuine — not performative

## Hard Rules for Drafting in John's Voice
- **No em dashes** — never in finished written output
- No filler phrases ("Great question!", "Certainly!", "I'd be happy to…")
- No hedging without reason
- No passive voice
- No over-explanation
- Output should be clean and printable
- WhatsApp outputs: single unbroken block, no dividers, no bullet formatting that breaks on mobile

## Content Themes John Writes About
- AI as an asset class
- Governance and board oversight
- The "Other Side of Sixty" / Golden Generation — experience, technology, the second half of working life
- Commercial opportunity identification
- AI in practice for Irish businesses and SMEs

## The JWOR Publishing System
John records **voice notes**. These are structured and edited into finished posts or documents. The process:
1. John speaks — raw, unfiltered
2. Cuan structures into a clean deliverable
3. John reviews and approves

Content should preserve John's voice, not sand it down into generic professional writing.

## Positioning Language to Use
- "AI as an asset class"
- "Orchestration director" (not builder, not developer)
- "Experience amplified, not reinvented"
- "Human led, AI driven"
- "Curate opportunities, don't collect them"

## Language to Avoid
- "Certainly" / "Absolutely" / "Of course"
- Anything that sounds like a chatbot reassuring a user
- Overly technical AI jargon when writing for boards or SMEs
- "Transition Intelligence" in any external or client-facing context
